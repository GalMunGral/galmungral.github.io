Research Software Engineering: A Philosophical Investigation

All human activities are embedded in a vast network of social relations. When we eat, we partake in a relation with the farmers who grew our food, the workers who processed and transported it, and the engineers who designed the machinery that produced it. When we drive, with the mechanical engineers who designed the vehicle, the civil engineers who built the roads and bridges, and the urban planners who laid out the city.

Here we must draw a distinction between action and activity. Consider singing: one can perform on stage, demonstrate technique to a student, or sing in a private shower — and while these could be carried out by the same person with the same techniques, they are categorically different activities, as they involve entirely different social relations — with an audience, a student, and oneself, respectively. What makes an activity what it is, then, is not the action alone but, more fundamentally, the social relations it serves. Software engineering is not unlike singing in this regard: what we do with software does not, on its own, define what we are doing. When the language we inherit draws no distinction between activities that share the same action but serve entirely different social relations, we risk treating them as the same — and it is precisely this confusion we must seek to untangle, before we can accurately define what our profession is and what it demands of us.

A Classification of Software

To examine these relations more precisely, we must first ask what software actually is. Here is one way to think of it: software is a mechanism that transforms data: grid specifications into a simulation run, raw data into visualizations and statistical summaries, or user intent into a new frame of pixels. A program is the textual description and prescription of such a mechanism, and a computer is a general-purpose machine that enacts any such mechanism by interpreting the program it is given. The authoring of such programs is the activity we call engineering.

The computer is, in Aristotle's terms, a first potentiality: a machine that could become any mechanism we prescribe, whose significance lies entirely in what it could become. A program is simultaneously a first actuality and a second potentiality: actual in that it exists as a readable, shareable, evaluable text, yet potential in that it holds within it only the capacity to transform inputs into outputs without yet having exercised it. A running process is the second actuality: the program fully at work, its capacity no longer merely held but exercised, actively transforming inputs into outputs in the world. The philosophically interesting thing about a program is precisely the dual nature of that middle term — it exists, it can be read and evaluated and demonstrated, and yet it has not fulfilled its telos — and it is this tension that gives rise to the distinctions we need.

From this structure we can identify two fundamentally different raisons d'être for software: expository and instrumental. This classification is not intrinsic to the software but derives from the social relations it partakes in.

Expository software enacts the social relation of author and audience. It necessarily achieves second actuality — the code must be run — but that second actuality is not in service of a need but of a proposition: the running either makes the claim directly — as in a visualization, where the output is the argument — or produces evidence in support of one, as when simulation results are included in a paper and the code as supplementary material. The raison d'être is rhetorical. Success means the argument is clear, the claim is convincing, the demonstration illuminates what it sets out to illuminate. Reliability and performance matter only insofar as they serve that purpose. A demonstration that fails before making its point has failed; one that makes its point and subsequently fails has succeeded.

Instrumental software enacts the social relation of producer and consumer. Second actuality is pursued to the fullest extent — the running process and the outputs it produces are what someone needs. Nobody cares that the program exists as a first actuality and second potentiality — apart from the software engineers, that is. The software stands or falls by the quality, reliability, and availability of what it produces. The raison d'être is productive. Success means correct and reliable output delivered. The obligations that follow are correspondingly stronger. In contrast to expository software, which operates at the level of ideas — where the consequences of failure are epistemic, a flawed argument or a misleading claim — instrumental software operates in the world, where the consequences of failure are immediate and material, with real effects on real lives. For simulations, correctness and reproducibility are paramount — others will build on the results, and errors propagate. For products, the bar is higher still: correctness, reliability, availability, performance, security, and maintainability all matter, because real people whose everyday lives are shaped to some extent by what is built are depending on what the software produces.

There is another dimension along which we can analyze the social relations software serves — the structure of the human need that underlies them. Some needs are recurrent: they can be fulfilled temporarily but never permanently, because they are conditions of our being. The need to eat, to sleep, to be entertained — these return inevitably, no act of fulfillment extinguishes them. Society gives rise to many more such persistent needs, on top of those of maintaining bodily functions — and this manifests in the need to play our role in society and carry out our job duties, which are not dissolved by doing so but return with each new day. The software industry is sustained by precisely these persistent needs — they are its economic foundation, enduring as long as society itself endures, and enabling business models like subscriptions and advertising that depend on the relation never being permanently dissolved. Other needs are contingent: the very act of fulfilling them dissolves them. The need to have a wedding is satisfied by having the wedding — it is not a condition of being, and one will not have more than a handful in a lifetime. Some software serves such contingent needs: a data processing script that cleans one particular dataset, a simulation run for a single research project — once the need is fulfilled, the relation dissolves — both the relation between the person and the software and the social relation that underlies it. We call software that serves contingent needs one-off, and software that serves persistent needs sustained.

Combining these two dimensions gives us four kinds of software:

  • One-off expository: e.g. visualizations (figures, videos), paper supplements.
  • One-off instrumental: e.g. data cleaning scripts, simulation runs, analysis pipelines.
  • Sustained expository: e.g. interactive visualizations, proof-of-concept systems.
  • Sustained instrumental: e.g. platforms, systems, tools, and pipelines in production.

It is worth pointing out that these categories are social constructs, not a metaphysical taxonomy — dictated by social relations rather than intrinsic properties, and as such they are fluid, shifting throughout the lifetime of the software as those relations shift — just as our own professional and personal identities are not fixed but constituted by the social relations we navigate, which themselves shift as our careers progress and our relations with others change. The same software can serve different social relations at different times — just as a singer can perform the same song on stage, at a rally, or in a lesson, each time in service of a different social relation. What is more, software can hold multiple classifications at the same moment — just as a person can hold multiple social relations simultaneously, each with its own obligations. A simulation run during the course of research, its outputs feeding into ongoing analysis, is one-off instrumental; the same simulation, used incidentally to generate figures for a paper, is simultaneously one-off expository. A proof-of-concept system is sustained expository, but as people come to depend on it and incorporate it into their workflows, it becomes simultaneously sustained instrumental. A one-off instrumental can also be embedded within a sustained instrumental — when its functionality is packaged and provided as a service that runs per user request, the one-off instrumental computations become part of a larger sustained instrumental system.

The Language Problem

Given this framework, we can now return to the question: what exactly distinguishes "research software engineering" from the unqualified "software engineering"? What does "research" really mean here, beyond its obvious literal sense? It is undeniable that the academic setting is a distinctive characteristic of our work — but it does not quite capture the essential challenges.

Just as understanding a word requires tracing its origins — the language game, in Wittgenstein's terms, that it was part of, the social relations that gave it meaning — understanding our profession requires understanding the software industry: the language game whose vocabulary we have inherited, and without which it loses all meaning. As we pointed out earlier, the software industry is mainly concerned with sustained instrumental software — and for good reason: the economic model is sustained by, and in turn actively incentivizes, the cultivation of stable, long-term relations between producers and consumers. This in turn dictates the entire superstructure of practices and values, from technical imperatives such as reliability, security, availability, and maintainability, to market imperatives such as user friendliness, growth, and engagement.

The funding model of academic research, by contrast, incentivizes outputs: prototypes, publications, results. It neither acknowledges nor directly incentivizes the cultivation of the requisite social relations, so it falls upon us to identify and maintain them in an appropriate manner, in the absence of any monetary incentive as a natural guidance, or sometimes even in the face of incentives that do the exact opposite. What is more, even when such relations do form and persist, they are rarely of the same kind as the industry's producer-consumer relation, and yet we inherit the industry's vocabulary and values as if they were. We work across the full range of relations that shift constantly, sometimes across projects, often within a single one. A one-off becomes a dependency, a proof-of-concept becomes infrastructure, and while we are fixated on the output, this silent shift in the nature of the activity goes undetected. To make things worse, the natural language we so rarely question does not offer us the ability to discern such nuances and subtleties, and when that shift goes unrecognized the consequences are real: misaligned expectations, wasted effort, frustration, burnout, people arguing past each other, each applying criteria that are perfectly reasonable in the social relation they imagine themselves to be in, and neither quite understanding why the other seems unreasonable.

The word that sits at the very center of this tension is "user". To see where the problem lies, let us take expository software as a case study. Who exactly is the "user" here, and what relation do we have with them? Upon examination, it becomes apparent that the so-called "user" has no existence outside of the linguistic realm — it is a kind of abstraction, a placeholder role that may not correspond to any actual person in the relevant social sense. Surely someone uses the software, one might reasonably object — a reviewer, perhaps, who runs the code to evaluate the claim. But as we have seen, the performance of the same action does not constitute the same activity, which is grounded in the social relation it serves. A reviewer might temporarily occupy the role, much as an actor inhabits a part in a play, and when the evaluation is complete, the performance ends and they return to their ordinary lives — the role does not persist beyond the context that gave rise to it. To characterize such a person as a "user" is a category error.

This is where the linguistic problem becomes especially consequential. In some languages there is no lexical distinction between blue and green, and speakers of those languages do not make the same perceptual distinctions that speakers of other languages do. Similarly, when our vocabulary offers no built-in distinction between a linguistic reality and an ontological one, we risk organizing our practices around a parasocial relation with a figment of imagination.

The central challenge of our profession is therefore not the mechanical execution of designing, coding, testing, and deploying software, nor the unexamined pursuit of virtues such as security, maintainability, correctness, or user friendliness — virtues that are real and important, but whose relevance is always relative to the social relations the software serves. To properly invest our limited time and effort, we must develop the diagnostic capacity to ask, before anything else: what relation does this software serve, and what does that relation demand of us? In a profession as young and as undefined as ours, no one will hand us answers to those questions — we must articulate them ourselves. And to articulate them, we must first invent the language.

As Wittgenstein said,

Die Grenzen meiner Sprache bedeuten die Grenzen meiner Welt.

To expand the limits of our world, we must first expand the limits of our language.
