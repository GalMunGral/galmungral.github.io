Critique of Research Software Engineering

Wenqi He · Research Software Engineer, NCSA, University of Illinois

All human activities are embedded in social relations. Whenever we put a foot on the pedal, we enter into a web of relations with others: the mechanical engineers who designed the vehicle, the civil engineers who built the roads beneath it, the urban planners who laid out the city. As such, we cannot analyze an area of human activity without taking into account its surrounding social relations, which, as impersonal as they might be, impose on us their own distinct norms and values. Even when the actions are the same, the activities are not — a singer can perform in front of an audience, demonstrate a technique to a student, or sing alone in the shower. What defines our professional activity, therefore, is not how we create software, but for whom and to what end.

We normally think of software as instrumental, its utility lying entirely in the output it produces from the inputs. But software is also an expressive medium: it can not only transform data but transmit ideas and further arguments. We may call this type of software rhetorical. Sometimes the output is the argument — a visualization makes a claim about data, a simulation produces results that validate a theory. But sometimes the running itself is the argument: a prototype that executes convinces the observer, in a way no description could, that the proposed system is technically feasible, and that the underlying idea has practical merit. In both cases the sole criterion is the effectiveness of the argument — reliability, performance, etc. matter only insofar as they contribute to it. A demonstration that crashes before making its point has failed; one that makes its point and subsequently crashes has, for all practical purposes, succeeded.

Apart from instrumental and rhetorical, we could also analyze software along another dimension: by whether the needs they address are lasting or transient. Some needs can be fulfilled temporarily, but no act of fulfillment extinguishes them, as they are conditions of our being: the need to eat, to sleep, to be entertained, to name but a few. Society gives rise to many more such needs beyond the merely biological — the need to carry out our duties, for instance, is never dissolved by carrying them out (or so we hope). Other needs are contingent: the very act of fulfilling them extinguishes them. The need to have a wedding, for example, is satisfied by simply having the wedding. Some software serves such contingent needs — a data processing script that cleans one particular dataset, a simulation run for a single research project — and once the need is fulfilled, the relation dissolves. We may call software that serves contingent needs ephemeral, and software that serves recurrent needs persistent.

Combining these two dimensions gives us four kinds of software:

  • Ephemeral-rhetorical: e.g. one-off scripts and programs written to produce figures, renderings, interface screenshots, paper supplements.
  • Ephemeral-instrumental: e.g. migration scripts, data cleaning scripts, analysis pipelines, simulation runs.
  • Persistent-rhetorical: e.g. research websites, teaching tools, live demos, interactive visualizations, proof-of-concept systems.
  • Persistent-instrumental: e.g. shared libraries, data pipelines, user-facing platforms, cyberinfrastructure.

These categories are by no means a fixed taxonomy. As social relations shift over time, so too do the categories we might assign to a piece of software — and moreover, multiple categories can apply at the same time. A simulation run in the course of research is ephemeral-instrumental; the same simulation, used to generate figures for a paper, is also ephemeral-rhetorical. A proof-of-concept system is persistent-rhetorical, but if people start to incorporate it into their workflows, it becomes, simultaneously, persistent-instrumental, and there are many more possible combinations.

Having laid out this framework, we can now ask more precisely: what exactly distinguishes “research software engineering” from just “software engineering”? For whom do we create software and to what end? To answer that, we must first understand the language game, or Sprachspiel, in Wittgenstein’s terms, that our language belongs to:

The software industry is, as a whole, mainly concerned with what we have categorized as persistent-instrumental software, as its underlying economic apparatus is sustained by, and actively incentivizes, the cultivation of stable, long-term — but not necessarily mutually beneficial — relations between producers and consumers.

The superstructure of practices and values — “reliability”, “security”, “availability”, “usability”, etc. — like deep-sea fish, cannot survive when trawled out of the deep-sea capitalism they inhabit. Capitalism requires rhetorical software as well, e.g. pitch demos to attract venture capital funding, vaporware to generate hype. But these are ancillary to the instrumental kind: before the money dries up, an actual persistent-instrumental product has to be produced so the company can sustain itself. There is, in other words, a reckoning.

Research software generally falls into the ephemeral-instrumental or persistent-instrumental category — simulation codes, scientific packages, cyberinfrastructure. But a lot of us also dedicate efforts to rhetorical software: prototypes, demos, visualizations, and the like. Unlike in industry, where cultivating enduring relations with consumers is an imperative, we work across short-term projects and grant cycles and are therefore rarely incentivized to produce anything truly persistent. Nor is there existential pressure to produce anything instrumental. The capitalist economy, if we put the moralist judgments aside, has at least a healthy feedback mechanism: create utility and you are rewarded financially to create more. Sell empty promises and you won't survive long past the seed round. You cannot game the market in the long run. In contrast, as there is no mechanism analogous to the market that constrains our work, our situation is, quite frankly, endless cash burning with no real payoff, with free money from government funding agencies, which are, in a sense, venture capitalists in state’s clothing — except that venture capitalists never take people’s money against their will.

Who, then, do we answer to, if not real people? At the end of the day, we are never rewarded by real people in exchange for the value we produce, only through the state as an intermediary in the name of “the people” for something we only promise to produce. And what is it that we promise? Oftentimes it is software of the rhetorical kind — prototypes, demos, etc. But, however sophisticated the prototype, however polished the demo, it is not a utility in itself, but merely a promise of utility, a rhetoric that aims to convince the audience of a utility, of which the prototype is merely a mirage. Here is the entire logic: we first make a promise of such a prototype, which is itself a promise of future “productionized” utility, and then we proceed to fulfill that promise of a promise by delivering that nested promise we promised to make. Is it a scam, then? Absolutely not! Sure, it looks like a scam, sounds like a scam, and quacks like a scam, but whether you believe it or not, it is definitely not a scam! The rules of the capitalist market do not bind us, so we don’t need to actually deliver anything to anyone. But then where do we get the money from? Don’t worry — the mighty and caring hand of the state apparatus will yank that cash from people’s pockets on our behalf, whether they like it or not.

If we never answer to real people, then how come we can conduct user research, write user stories, design user interfaces, and create user experiences? Who are these users, if not real people? And what relation do we have with them? The truth is, if we search outside of language, the so-called “user” is nowhere to be found. It is an improper definite description, in Russell’s terms, like the bald king of France: apparently meaningful, but with no real referent in the world. Following Russell’s analysis, “the king of France is bald” is false not because there are no bald people, but because there is no king of France. Similarly, “the user wants X” is false not because there is no person who wants X. It is certainly not true that no one wants X — in fact, there is at least one person who wants X: the very person who claims that “the user” wants X, much like a ventriloquist speaking through a dummy, or a politician who “represents” the electorate and makes policies “in their interests.” It is false because there is no user.

Surely the reviewer uses the software, and is therefore, by definition, a user? But singing in the shower does not make you a singer in any real sense of the word. In the language game of the software industry, “user” is never simply used in the sense found in a dictionary, but always refers to a real socioeconomic participant embedded in a bona fide producer-consumer relation. In contrast, when the aim of a piece of software is to convince a reviewer, it is rhetorical in nature, and the reviewer is really an audience rather than a consumer — what the industry would call a “user.” A reviewer might temporarily occupy the role of a “user” as a narrative device, much as an actor plays a part, but when the evaluation is complete, the performance ends and they return to their ordinary lives. A reviewer clicking a button is therefore only superficially the same action as a paying user clicking a button. The sociological and psychological forces at play are entirely different. The interaction between the human and the software may appear identical, but the interaction between humans through the software is of an entirely different kind. To equate the two is a category error.

One might object: where is the harm in a little creative imagination? And that is indeed a valid point. After all, the best king in all of history is one without subjects, for there is never a revolt, a protest, or even so much as the slightest grievance against him. And the most beautiful lady in the world is, as no knight would dare dispute, mi señora, la sin par Dulcinea del Toboso. But we should not be reassured, or even proud, of how innocuous our fantasies are. We should ask instead: what good does it do? If all we do is pour our creativity into make-believe, then perhaps we should consider ourselves part of the entertainment industry.

So where does that leave those of us who create rhetorical software for a living? What are we, if not parasites on the society that sustains us? And what about art, music, literature, mathematics, philosophy, and much of high culture — are they any different? Who does a museum, a library, or an art gallery serve? Exactly what value do they provide? Why is the government squandering taxpayers’ hard-earned money on such endeavors, when it could be investing in companies that actually create persistent-instrumental software providing real value to real users? After all, it’s user research that we conduct, user stories that we write, user interfaces that we create, user experiences that we design, and it’s users that we worship.

So much for the “sociological-philosophical critique.” In case it was not obvious, the preceding section was a deliberate enactment of the capitalist language game we sought to understand — and the fact that it wasn’t obvious is precisely the point. Just like that, the ideology sneaked into the analysis without you, the audience, noticing, which is exactly what is so insidious about it.

Language, as it turns out, is not so innocuous after all. The words we use — “user”, “production”, “release”, and so on — are part of an ideology that grew out of the capitalist mode of production, and like all ideology, parasitic in our consciousness. Through instrumentalization, capitalism perverts design and engineering, turning them against the very art and science they owe so much to. We never notice we are playing capitalism’s language game because the game has become the only game we know, and, as Wittgenstein said,

die Grenzen meiner Sprache bedeuten die Grenzen meiner Welt.

But all hope is not lost. We are not bound to capitalism, as much as capitalism condemns us. The first step is to stop playing its language game. There is no denial that we are socioeconomic beings, but we are much more than that. There are ideas intrinsically worth articulating, arguments intrinsically worth making, values intrinsically worth defending, regardless of whether anyone benefits from them, monetarily or otherwise. Does it matter that two black holes were merging a billion light years away, their gravity distorting the very fabric of spacetime, even though there is no practical socioeconomic implication whatsoever? Of course it does! Being able to breathe in the cracks of the socioeconomic apparatus is not a condemnation but a privilege, so that we get to stare at things so much more important than the materialist life that capitalism so values.

And so we topple the “user” from the pedestal, and the value system along with it. We don’t mourn their death. We write stories and poems, proofs and equations, conjure visions and sounds, for curious people in the audience, conveying the truth and beauty that we hold dear. There is much we can still offer, with software as a rhetorical device, as research software engineers.
