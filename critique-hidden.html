Critique of Research Software Engineering

Wenqi He · Research Software Engineer, NCSA, University of Illinois

All human activities are embedded in social relations. Whenever we put a foot on the pedal, we enter into a web of relations with others: the mechanical engineers who designed the vehicle, the civil engineers who built the roads beneath it, the urban planners who laid out the city. As such, we cannot analyze an area of human activity without taking into account its surrounding social relations, which, as impersonal as they might be, impose on us their own distinct norms and values. Even when the actions are the same, the activities are not — a singer can perform in front of an audience, demonstrate a technique to a student, or sing alone in the shower. What defines our professional activity, therefore, is not how we create software, but for whom and to what end.

The software industry is, as a whole, mainly concerned with persistent-instrumental software, as its underlying economic apparatus is sustained by, and actively incentivizes, the cultivation of stable, long-term — but not necessarily mutually beneficial — relations between producers and consumers. The superstructure of practices and values — “reliability”, “security”, “availability”, “usability”, etc. — like deep-sea fish, cannot survive when trawled out of the deep-sea capitalism they inhabit. Capitalism requires rhetorical software as well, e.g. pitch demos to attract venture capital funding, vaporware to generate hype. But these are ancillary to the instrumental kind: before the money dries up, an actual persistent-instrumental product has to be produced so the company can sustain itself. There is, in other words, a reckoning.

Research software generally falls into the ephemeral-instrumental or persistent-instrumental category — simulation codes, scientific packages, cyberinfrastructure. But a lot of us also dedicate efforts to rhetorical software: prototypes, demos, visualizations, and the like. Unlike in industry, where cultivating enduring relations with consumers is an imperative, we work across short-term projects and grant cycles and are therefore rarely incentivized to produce anything truly persistent. Nor is there existential pressure to produce anything instrumental. The capitalist economy, if we put the moralist judgments aside, has at least a healthy feedback mechanism: create utility and you are rewarded financially to create more. Sell empty promises and you won’t survive long past the seed round. You cannot game the market in the long run. In contrast, as there is no mechanism analogous to the market that constrains our work, our situation is, quite frankly, endless cash burning with no real payoff, with free money from government funding agencies, which are, in a sense, venture capitalists in state’s clothing — except that venture capitalists never take people’s money against their will.

Who, then, do we answer to, if not real people? We are never rewarded by real people in exchange for the value we produce, only through the state as an intermediary in the name of “the people” for something we only promise to produce. And what is it that we promise? Often it is software of the rhetorical kind — prototypes, demos, etc. But, however sophisticated the prototype, however polished the demo, it is not a utility in itself, but merely a promise of utility, a rhetoric that aims to convince the audience of a utility, of which the prototype is merely a mirage. Here is the entire logic: we first make a promise of such a prototype, which is itself a promise of future “productionized” utility, and then we proceed to fulfill that promise of a promise by delivering that nested promise we promised to make. Is it a scam, then? Absolutely not! Sure, it looks like a scam, sounds like a scam, and quacks like a scam, but whether you believe it or not, it is definitely not a scam! The rules of the capitalist market do not bind us, so we don’t need to actually deliver anything to anyone. But then where do we get the money from? Don’t worry — the mighty and caring hand of the state apparatus will yank that cash from people’s pockets on our behalf, whether they like it or not.

If we never answer to real people, then how come we can conduct user research, write user stories, design user interfaces, and create user experiences? Who are these users, if not real people? And what relation do we have with them? The truth is, if we search outside of language, the so-called “user” is nowhere to be found. It is an improper definite description, in Russell’s terms, like the bald king of France: apparently meaningful, but with no real referent in the world. “The king of France is bald” is false not because there are no bald people, but because there is no king of France. Similarly, “the user wants X” is false not because there is no person who wants X — in fact, there is at least one person who wants X: the very person who claims that “the user” wants X, much like a ventriloquist speaking through a dummy, or a politician who “represents” the electorate and makes policies “in their interests.” It is false because there is no user.

Surely the reviewer uses the software, and is therefore, by definition, a user? But singing in the shower does not make you a singer in any real sense of the word. In the software industry, “user” is never simply used in the sense found in a dictionary, but always refers to a real socioeconomic participant embedded in a bona fide producer-consumer relation. In contrast, when the aim of a piece of software is to convince a reviewer, it is rhetorical in nature, and the reviewer is really an audience rather than a consumer. A reviewer clicking a button is therefore only superficially the same action as a paying user clicking a button. The sociological and psychological forces at play are entirely different. The interaction between the human and the software may appear identical, but the interaction between humans through the software is of an entirely different kind. To equate the two is a category error.

One might object: where is the harm in a little creative imagination? And that is indeed a valid point. After all, the best king in all of history is one without subjects, for there is never a revolt, a protest, or even so much as the slightest grievance against him. And the most beautiful lady in the world is, as no knight would dare dispute, mi señora, la sin par Dulcinea del Toboso.

But we should not be reassured, or even proud, of how innocuous our fantasies are. We should ask instead: what good does it do? If all we do is squander our creativity on make-believe, then we are nothing but entertainers. But people enjoy entertainment and willingly pay for shows, whereas we rely on the government as the muscle to shake them down for a show that isn’t very good, that they probably never get to see, and wouldn’t want to even if they could. So what are we, if not parasites on the society that sustains us?

But what if the show turns out to be brilliant — beautiful, even?

Does it matter that two black holes were merging a billion light years away, their gravity distorting the very fabric of spacetime, the waves passing through every millimeter, every micrometer, every nanometer of your body at this very moment, while you are reading this text, even though there is no practical socioeconomic implication whatsoever? Now you could either ask “what good does it do?” and with that dwell in the utter lack of meaning of your career. Or you could respond: of course it does.

You topple the “user” from the pedestal, and the capitalist ideology along with it. You don’t mourn their death. You go on to write stories and poems, proofs and equations, conjure visions and sounds, for curious people in the audience, even if there is none, conveying the truth and beauty that you hold dear. There is so much you can still offer, wielding the fantastical rhetorical device that is software.

Only as an aesthetic phenomenon are existence and the world eternally justified.

— Nietzsche, The Birth of Tragedy
