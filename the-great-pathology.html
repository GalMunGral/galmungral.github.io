The Great Pathology

Wenqi He · Research Software Engineer, NCSA, University of Illinois

nur als ästhetisches Phänomen ist das Dasein und die Welt ewig gerechtfertigt.

— Nietzsche, Die Geburt der Tragödie

We are a creative species. Except we don’t really create — not in the fullest sense. We don’t create matter or life. We transform them from one form to another. Ideas, though — those we do seem to genuinely create. Which is either a power shared with God, or gods, or aliens somewhere out there, or with no other beings at all, depending on who you ask.

Material things are either consumable or durable. Either way there’s a definite objective, and once achieved, the thing is consumed and destroyed or decays through time. Life is similar — it has a shape it’s growing into. Even the oldest tree knows when to stop.

Software doesn’t. That’s the problem.

We call ourselves engineers, but the analogy only goes so far. Most software grows as requirements change, and unlike a bridge or a loaf of bread, it’s never consumed and never completed. Satisfy one need and you’ve just uncovered the next. It accumulates, compounds, grows like a living organism — except a pathological one. A cancer. No limit to its growth, no terminal shape it’s growing into, until you remove it surgically or it keeps growing and kills the host.

And here’s the thing I keep coming back to: this isn’t ultimately a problem about the software. It’s a problem about the people who have to hold it in their heads. The object is pure thought — formal constructs, ideas all the way down. And because it’s liberated from the physical world while we are not — while we remain stuck in our wet, carbon-based, embarrassingly limited brains — the tension is intractable. Software isn’t alone in this: law, mathematics, any field that builds and maintains a coherent conceptual system shares the same nature.

Are we doomed then? Well, yes. The loss of coherence looms on the horizon the way organ failure looms for even the healthiest person. What we can do is not unlike what we do with our own lives: prolong it, consciously or not, until one day we can’t anymore.

As Silenus told King Midas when finally caught — the best thing for man is not to have been born. The second best is to die soon. We could refuse to build when existing software suffices, reject feature requests, kill a project before it kills us. But if we must participate in what we know to be a death sentence, we can at least give it the dignity of swift execution rather than slow metastasis. This requires discipline — resistance to growth at every turn. Software architecture, design patterns, best practices — all of it follows naturally once you’ve genuinely internalized what you’re dealing with, not as rules but as instincts. You start to feel the weight of every addition.

AI didn’t create this problem and hasn’t solved it. The tension between the unbounded nature of ideas and the very bounded nature of the minds that must hold them is untouched. But AI accelerates the confrontation with the inevitable. Inheriting a codebase you cannot understand or maintain is like being told you have five days to live instead of fifty years. The outcome is always the same, but your experience of reality changes. It strips away the delusion that there was ever any hope to begin with.
